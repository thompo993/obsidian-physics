
### discovery of the muon 
The [[muon]] is a fundamental particle with some unique properties, it was discovered 1936 by Carl Anderson and Seth Neddermeyer. They determined the mass charge ratio of a mystery particle, which turned out to be the fundamental particle the muon, which had some interesting properties. 


|       | Charge  | Spin | Mass        | Lifetime ($\mu$) |
| ----- | ------- | ---- | ----------- | ---------------- |
| e     | $\pm$ e | 1/2  | m$_{e}$     | $\infty$         |
| $\mu$ | $\pm$ e | 1/2  | 207m$_{e}$  | 2.197            |
| p     | $\pm$ e | 1/2  | 1836m$_{e}$ | $\infty$         |

The muon was confused for the Yukawa meson, but its relatively weak interaction with matter was inconsistent with theory. After the discovery of the muon, the critical muon life cycle equation was determined: 
$$
\pi ^{\pm} \rightarrow \mu^{\pm} \rightarrow e^{\pm}
$$
### First application of the muon
The muon has a relatively long lifespan of 2.197$\mu s$ This meant that it could be used to demonstrate general relativity. Bruno Rossi and David Hall measured rates of muons at high and low altitudes, and found that the change in muon lifetime increased with momentum as predicted by [[Einstein]]. 

### Muon perspectives 

The particle physics view of muons is based of the [[Standard Model of Particle Physics]]. The muon and the associated muon neutrinos are recognized as members of the lepton family. The muon is subject to the electromagnetic interaction and the weak interaction. The decay process is governed by the weak interaction: 
$$
\mu ^+ \rightarrow \nu _e \quad + \quad \bar{\nu} _\mu
$$
They key aspect of the weak force is that [[parity]] is not conserved in the weak decay processes. This parity non-conservation is what leads to the negative helicity of neutrinos, and to the antisymmetric [[positron]] emissions of the muon.  Virtually all muons follow this decay path, therefore muons are very efficient for the production of [[neutrinos]], so often muon sources send of neutrinos to distant facilities that detect neutrinos, such as JPARC. 

At the LHC muons were tracked as they are a significant decay productions of the [[Higgs Boson]].
Muons may also be used to push the energy frontier of particle physics, as they have less radiative energy loss compared to electrons. They are also being investigated for their departure from the muon g faction from theory, which will reveal key insight into the standard model. 

All muons used in [[MuSR]] are 100% polarised, as we use magnets to select for muons produced from pions that come to rest on the surface of the thin carbon target, called surface muons. This allows the initial and final spin to be known, which allows for MuSR to work. 

### Implanted Muons in matter 
To become implanted in a sample from where they have to decay, muons must be degraded, as to slow them down. This can be done by placing a thick sample in the muon beam, or some other material in front of the sample.  A key note is that the muons only interact with coulombic degrees of freedom, they don't loose their polarization at all. 

Muon states are are often classified into two groups **diamagnetic** and **paramagnetic**, which refers to the magnetic nature of the electrons to which the muons are coupled. (Definitions of [[Diamagnetism]] and [[Paramagnetism]] can be found on these backlinks)

 - **Diamagnetic Muon State:** This occurs when the muon is uncoupled to any unpaired electron density (its just chilling on its own). This could be due to strength of coupling to nearby electrons, or because it has coupled to two electrons which are in the singlet state
 - **Paramagnetic Muon State:** This occurs when the muon is strongly bound to a single unpaired electron. 
The nature of the final muon state depends on the sample that is being investigated. In metals, the final muon state usually remains a positive muon, a diamagnetic muon state. In insulators, the diamagnetic positive muon state is one possibility, but another state retaining an unpaired electron is also possible, a diamagnetic state. The simplest form a paramagnetic state is muonium, a muon analogue of the neutral hydrogen atom. These different state types respond to a externally applied transverse field (TF, a field applied perpendicular to the initial muon state). diamagnetic states produce a muon precession frequency that is very close that of the  bare muon, but paramagnetic states produce a significant shift in muon precession frequency.

### Condensed matter physics

Condensed matter physics is a key focus of muon spectroscopy. it uses the implanted muons as local probes of static and dynamic magnetism in the target sample. The muon is typically regarded as a local probe with a limited set of specific stopping sites. 

The muon can also be used to effectively "randomly sample" the internal magnetic environment within a sample. A typical example would be superconducting (see [[superconductivity]]) vortex lattices having a long length scale compared to any individual sites in the lattice. 

Besides studying static magnetism, muons can be used to study the fluctuating magnetic fields associated with various dynamical precesses in the sample (see [[larmor precession]]) . the range of fluctuations rates that can be observed using the muons are illustrated below,/
![[fig-muon-sensititivy-to-dynamics-s-blundell.png|269]]
**above:** Muon sensitivity to dynamics compared for continuous and pulsed sources and for three different values of coupling: 0.2, 20, and 2000 MHz. These reflect typical values for nuclear dipolar coupling, moderate electronic coupling, and very strong electronic coupling. The lower band is the slow fluctuation regime and the upper band is the fast fluctuation regime.


one factor is the coupling constant A, (measured in frequency units.) linking the muon to internal magnetic fields that are fluctuating at a rate of $\nu$. When the fluctuations are slow, so that $\nu < A$ the relaxation rate $\lambda$ of the muon-spin polarisation is proportional to $\nu$ 

In the opposite limit of fast fluctuations, where $\nu > A$ the muon spin relaxation rate is proportional to $\frac{A^2}{\nu}$ .

A second factor determining the limits of dynamical sensitivity is a range of muon relaxation rates that can be measured by a given muon spectrometer. With a maximum relaxation rate $\lambda_{max}$ determined by the resolution of the source and detectors. Minimum relaxation rate  $\lambda_{min}$ is determined by the background at a continuous source and by the muon flux, and detector dead time at a pulsed source. 

This range of measurable relaxation rates define two bands of sensitivity, one for the slow-fluctuation limit and one for the fast fluctuation limit. the gap between the two bands is called the wipe-out region, and is where the fluctuation leads to a relaxation that is too fast for the experimental apparatus and the signal is partially or totally missing.  

MuSR has several advantages compared to bulk magnetic studies, a volume-averaged probe of MuSR is relatively immune to low concentration immunity phases that can dominate bulk susceptibility measurements at low temperature. Phase segregated samples can be easily identified in muon data. 

MuSR is also well suited to studying anti-ferromagnetism, and allows measurements to be made in zero applied field. Another feature is that it has high sensitivity to very small magnetic fields (below 10 $\mu T$) and moments (below 0.1 $\mu_B$). The muons can penetrate both walls and windows of a [[cryostat]] which gives it great compatibility to low temperature measurements.
